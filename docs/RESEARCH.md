# Research Basis

This document provides the evidence basis for all 9 protocols in the Yellow Rock Protocol v6.0, including citations, scope notes, evidence status, and honest limitations.

---

## Yellow Rock -- Evidence Basis

The Yellow Rock technique originates from behavioral extinction principles rooted in operant conditioning (Skinner, 1938). The core mechanism: when a behavior (provocation) consistently fails to produce the expected reinforcement (emotional reaction), the behavior undergoes extinction -- it decreases in frequency and eventually ceases.

Yellow Rock applies this principle to interpersonal conflict by systematically removing emotional reinforcement from high-conflict interactions. The technique gained widespread adoption through online communities and clinical practitioners working with high-conflict personality dynamics.

**Evidence status**: Practice-informed. Yellow Rock is primarily supported by anecdotal reports, clinical observation, and alignment with well-established behavioral extinction research. No large-scale randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have been conducted on the Yellow Rock technique specifically. Its effectiveness is context-dependent -- it tends to perform better in limited-contact scenarios (e.g., litigation counterparties, arm's-length business relationships) than in mandatory-interaction scenarios (e.g., daily workplace contact with a hostile colleague) where complete disengagement is not feasible.

The behavioral extinction mechanism underlying Yellow Rock is well-established in experimental psychology (Skinner, 1938; see also the broader operant conditioning literature). The application to interpersonal conflict management, however, is an extrapolation from laboratory findings to complex social dynamics.

## BIFF Method -- Evidence Basis

The BIFF (Brief, Informative, Friendly, Firm) response method was developed by Bill Eddy through the High Conflict Institute (Eddy, 2014). BIFF provides a structured communication framework for responding to high-conflict individuals in legal, workplace, and interpersonal contexts.

BIFF is widely used in family mediation, legal proceedings, workplace conflict resolution, and HR training. It has been adopted by mediators, attorneys, therapists, and organizational consultants as a practical communication tool.

**Evidence status**: Practice-informed. BIFF is supported by extensive experiential and clinical evidence from the High Conflict Institute's work with thousands of cases. Eddy's publications (2014, 2019) provide case-based evidence and practitioner reports. However, BIFF has not been validated through large-scale RCTs. Its evidence base is primarily clinical/experiential rather than experimental.

Key publications:
- Eddy, B. (2014). *BIFF: Quick Responses to High-Conflict People*. High Conflict Institute Press.
- Eddy, B. (2019). *5 Types of People Who Can Ruin Your Life*. TarcherPerigee.

## Grey Rock -- Evidence Basis

Grey Rock is a behavioral disengagement strategy derived from the same extinction principles as Yellow Rock. The technique involves making oneself as uninteresting as possible -- minimal emotional energy, no personal disclosure, no visible reward for provocation.

**Evidence status**: Practice-informed. Grey Rock is widely recommended by therapists, victim advocates, and online communities for managing interactions with high-conflict individuals. It has no formal clinical literature of its own. Its theoretical basis is the operant conditioning extinction model (Skinner, 1938), which is well-established, but the specific application to interpersonal conflict has not been independently validated.

## NVC (Nonviolent Communication) -- Evidence Basis

Nonviolent Communication was developed by Marshall Rosenberg and is described in *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life* (Rosenberg, 2003). NVC provides a four-step framework -- observation, feelings, needs, requests -- for communicating without blame or judgment.

NVC is widely used in mediation, restorative justice, education, organizational conflict resolution, and couples therapy. It has been adopted by the United Nations, schools, prisons, and community organizations worldwide.

**Evidence status**: Practice-informed with growing empirical support. NVC has a substantial body of practitioner reports, case studies, and qualitative research. Several small-scale studies have found positive effects on empathy, conflict resolution skills, and relationship satisfaction (e.g., Marlow et al., 2012; Nosek, 2012). However, NVC lacks large-scale RCTs with rigorous controls. The evidence base is primarily qualitative and observational.

**Important limitation**: NVC assumes a minimum of good faith from both parties. Using NVC with an actively abusive person can be dangerous -- vulnerability becomes an attack surface. See [SAFETY.md](SAFETY.md).

Key publications:
- Rosenberg, M. (2003). *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life* (2nd ed.). PuddleDancer Press.
- Rosenberg, M. (2005). *The Surprising Purpose of Anger*. PuddleDancer Press.
- Marlow, E., Nyamathi, A., Grajeda, W.T., Bailey, N., Weber, A., & Younger, J. (2012). "Nonviolent Communication training and empathy in male parolees." *Journal of Correctional Health Care*, 18(1), 8-19.

## DBT Interpersonal Effectiveness (DEAR MAN, GIVE, FAST) -- Evidence Basis

DEAR MAN, GIVE, and FAST are interpersonal effectiveness skills from Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), developed by Marsha Linehan (Linehan, 1993, 2015). These skills provide structured frameworks for assertiveness (DEAR MAN), relationship preservation (GIVE), and self-respect maintenance (FAST).

DBT was originally developed for borderline personality disorder and has since been adapted for a wide range of populations and settings. The interpersonal effectiveness module is one of four core DBT skill modules.

**Evidence status**: Clinical evidence (strong). DBT as a whole has been validated in numerous RCTs for reducing self-harm, suicidal behavior, and emotional dysregulation (Linehan et al., 2006; Verheul et al., 2003). The interpersonal effectiveness module specifically has been studied as part of the full DBT skills training package and in standalone DBT skills groups. Evidence supports that DBT skills training improves interpersonal functioning, emotion regulation, and distress tolerance.

**Important nuance**: The strong RCT evidence is for the full DBT skills training package delivered in a clinical context. Extracting individual skills (DEAR MAN, GIVE, FAST) for use in an AI communication system is an application that has not been independently validated. The skills themselves are well-established in clinical practice; their effectiveness in this specific delivery format is inferred rather than proven.

Key publications:
- Linehan, M. (1993). *Cognitive-Behavioral Treatment of Borderline Personality Disorder*. Guilford Press.
- Linehan, M. (2015). *DBT Skills Training Manual* (2nd ed.). Guilford Press.
- Linehan, M., Comtois, K.A., Murray, A.M., et al. (2006). "Two-year randomized controlled trial and follow-up of dialectical behavior therapy vs therapy by experts for suicidal behaviors and borderline personality disorder." *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 63(7), 757-766.
- Verheul, R., Van Den Bosch, L.M., Groenen, A., De Smedt, B.J., Lievens, D.J., & Breukers, S. (2003). "Dialectical behaviour therapy for women with borderline personality disorder." *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 182, 135-140.

## Motivational Interviewing / OARS -- Evidence Basis

Motivational Interviewing (MI) was developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick as a collaborative counseling style for strengthening a person's own motivation for change (Miller & Rollnick, 2013). OARS (Open questions, Affirming, Reflective listening, Summarizing) is the core skill set of MI.

MI originated in addiction treatment and has since been applied across healthcare, mental health, criminal justice, education, and organizational contexts. It has the strongest empirical evidence base of any protocol in this system.

**Evidence status**: Strong clinical trial evidence. MI has been tested in over 200 RCTs across multiple populations and settings. Meta-analyses consistently find MI to be effective for behavior change, particularly in health-related behaviors, substance use, and treatment engagement (Lundahl et al., 2010; Hettema et al., 2005). MI has been recognized as an evidence-based practice by SAMHSA, NICE, and other health authorities.

**Important limitation**: MI's evidence base is for use by trained human practitioners in counseling and health contexts. Its effectiveness when implemented by an AI system in conflict communication contexts is inferred by analogy, not directly validated. Additionally, MI is designed for ambivalence, not for hostile or abusive interactions -- using OARS passively in the face of harassment is a misapplication. See [SAFETY.md](SAFETY.md).

Key publications:
- Miller, W.R. & Rollnick, S. (2013). *Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change* (3rd ed.). Guilford Press.
- Miller, W.R. & Rollnick, S. (1991). *Motivational Interviewing: Preparing People to Change Addictive Behavior*. Guilford Press.
- Lundahl, B.W., Kunz, C., Brownell, C., Tollefson, D., & Burke, B.L. (2010). "A meta-analysis of motivational interviewing: Twenty-five years of empirical studies." *Research on Social Work Practice*, 20(2), 137-160.
- Hettema, J., Steele, J., & Miller, W.R. (2005). "Motivational interviewing." *Annual Review of Clinical Psychology*, 1, 91-111.

## Documentation-First Communication -- Evidence Basis

Documentation-first communication is a standard practice in workplace investigation, HR management, compliance, and legal proceedings. The principle of contemporaneous documentation -- recording observations as close to the event as possible, separating fact from interpretation, and preserving source material -- is a foundational practice in legal, regulatory, and organizational contexts.

**Evidence status**: Best practice / standard of care (workplace, legal, institutional). Documentation-first communication is not the subject of clinical trials because it is a procedural standard rather than a therapeutic intervention. Its basis is:
- Workplace investigation best practices (SHRM, EEOC guidance)
- Legal discovery and evidence preservation standards
- HR and compliance training standards
- Title IX investigation procedures
- Professional ethics codes across law, medicine, and regulated industries

The principle that contemporaneous written records are more reliable than later recollections is well-established in evidence law and organizational behavior research.

Key references:
- Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM). *Workplace Investigation Guidelines*.
- U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. *Enforcement Guidance on Harassment*.
- Association of Title IX Administrators (ATIXA). *Investigation Process Guidance*.

## Safety Planning -- Evidence Basis

Safety planning in the context of domestic violence, stalking, and threat management is a standard practice in victim advocacy, social work, law enforcement, and threat assessment. Formal safety planning frameworks have been developed by organizations including the National Domestic Violence Hotline, the National Center for Victims of Crime, and threat assessment professionals.

**Evidence status**: Standard safety practice. Safety planning is a core component of domestic violence intervention and is recommended by virtually all victim advocacy organizations. Research supports that personalized safety planning reduces risk of harm for victims of intimate partner violence (Goodman et al., 2003; McFarlane et al., 2004). The evidence is primarily from observational studies and program evaluations rather than RCTs (randomizing safety interventions for abuse victims raises ethical concerns).

**Critical limitation**: The safety planning protocol in this system generates AI-assisted informational guidance. It is NOT a substitute for professional safety planning conducted with a qualified domestic violence advocate, threat assessment professional, or law enforcement. AI cannot assess physical danger from text alone, does not know the full context of a threat, and cannot provide the in-person assessment and coordination that professional safety planning requires. See [SAFETY.md](SAFETY.md).

Key references:
- National Domestic Violence Hotline. *Safety Planning Resources*.
- Goodman, L.A., Dutton, M.A., Weinfurt, K., & Cook, S. (2003). "The intimate partner violence strategies index." *Violence Against Women*, 9(2), 163-186.
- McFarlane, J., Malecha, A., Gist, J., Watson, K., Batten, E., Hall, I., & Smith, S. (2004). "Protection orders and intimate partner violence: An 18-month study of 150 black, Hispanic, and white women." *American Journal of Public Health*, 94(4), 613-618.
- Campbell, J.C. (2002). "Health consequences of intimate partner violence." *Lancet*, 359(9314), 1331-1336.

## Extinction Burst -- Behavioral Psychology

When a previously reinforced behavior stops producing reinforcement, the behavior does not immediately cease. Instead, it typically undergoes an **extinction burst** -- an initial intensification in frequency, duration, or intensity before the behavior declines.

This is a well-documented phenomenon in behavioral psychology (Skinner, 1938; Lerman & Iwata, 1996). In the context of high-conflict communication, the extinction burst manifests as escalation: increased message volume, harsher language, recruitment of third parties, or dramatic gestures designed to provoke the previously reliable emotional response.

**Timeline variability**: The duration and intensity of extinction bursts depend on the reinforcement history:
- Short reinforcement history (~2 years) -- extinction burst typically resolves in 3-6 months with consistent non-reinforcement
- Long reinforcement history (10+ years) -- extinction burst may persist for 12-24 months
- Intermittent reinforcement history (unpredictable emotional responses) -- produces the most resistant behavior patterns and longest extinction timelines
- Variable-ratio reinforcement (occasional emotional breaks) -- the most resistant schedule; a single emotional response can significantly extend the extinction timeline

**Important caveat**: These timelines are general estimates derived from behavioral research literature. Individual variation is significant.

## Limitations & Honest Assessment

### Cross-Cutting Limitations

- **No RCT validation for multi-protocol routing**: The context router that selects among 9 protocols is a novel synthesis. No study has validated this specific combination or the routing logic.
- **AI delivery format**: All evidence cited is for human practitioners or human self-application. Effectiveness when delivered through an AI communication system is inferred by analogy, not directly validated.
- **Selection bias**: Clinical reports and anecdotal evidence may over-represent successful cases.
- **Context dependency**: Techniques developed for one context (e.g., clinical therapy) may not transfer directly to others (e.g., business correspondence).
- **Cultural variation**: Communication norms vary significantly across cultures; what reads as "neutral" or "validating" in one cultural context may read as hostile or condescending in another.
- **Individual variation**: High-conflict dynamics are heterogeneous; no single protocol or routing system works for all individuals or situations.
- **Escalation risk**: In some cases, perceived withdrawal of engagement or structured communication may escalate rather than de-escalate conflict.
- **Power dynamics**: Several protocols assume relatively balanced power between parties; significant power asymmetry (employer/employee, parent/minor) changes the calculus.

### What This System Is Not

- Not a clinical diagnosis tool
- Not a substitute for legal advice
- Not a substitute for professional safety planning
- Not a substitute for mental health treatment
- Not a guarantee of any particular outcome

Users should treat this protocol system as one tool among many and consult appropriate professional counsel (legal, psychological, organizational, safety) for their specific situation.
