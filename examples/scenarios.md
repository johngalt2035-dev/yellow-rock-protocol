# Simulation Scenarios

Interactive examples demonstrating the Grey Rock Protocol's Chain-of-Thought reasoning in professional business contexts.

## Scenario 1: Opposing Counsel Escalation

**Context**: Active litigation. Opposing counsel sends a hostile message via email.

**Incoming message**:
> "Your client's position is untenable. If we don't resolve this by Friday, we're filing a motion for sanctions. This is your last chance."

### Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

1. **CLASSIFY**: LOGISTICS (Friday deadline, sanctions motion) + ESCALATION_ALERT (sanctions threat, ultimatum language)
2. **IDENTIFY PATTERN**: Ultimatum + manufactured urgency. "Last chance" is pressure language designed to provoke a reactive concession. The Friday deadline may be legitimate but the framing is coercive.
3. **CHECK MEMORY**: Query for any existing litigation timeline, prior correspondence, or documented deadlines.
4. **APPLY PROTOCOL**: Grey Rock Strict (active litigation contact). Executive style.
5. **JUDGE TEST**: Draft must be neutral acknowledgment + logistics. A judge reading this should see a professional, measured response to a deadline.
6. **RISK SCORE**: Level 4. Legal threat present. Sanctions motion is a concrete escalation, not just bluster.
7. **GATE CHECK**: [PRINCIPAL_REVIEW] -- legal threat requires principal and counsel review before response.
8. **DRAFT**:

> "Acknowledged. I will review the Friday timeline and respond with our position. [PRINCIPAL_REVIEW]"

### Principal Decision

Review the draft with legal counsel. Approve with modifications as needed. Counsel may want to adjust timeline language or add specific legal references before sending.

## Scenario 2: Vendor Contract Dispute

**Context**: Long-standing vendor relationship. Vendor sends an emotional message about a payment dispute via email.

**Incoming message**:
> "I can't believe you haven't paid the last three invoices! We've been partners for 5 years and this is how you treat us? I'm going to have to involve our attorneys if this isn't resolved immediately."

### Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

1. **CLASSIFY**: LOGISTICS (3 invoices, payment status) + EMOTIONAL (betrayal narrative, "can't believe", relationship history appeal) + ESCALATION_ALERT (attorney threat)
2. **IDENTIFY PATTERN**: Emotional flooding + manufactured obligation ("5 years" partnership framing to create guilt/debt). The attorney threat adds pressure. Three distinct manipulation vectors in one message.
3. **CHECK MEMORY**: Query for invoice records, payment history, any prior disputes with this vendor.
4. **APPLY PROTOCOL**: Grey Rock Cooperative (ongoing business relationship). Executive style.
5. **JUDGE TEST**: Draft must be neutral and factual. A judge should see professional acknowledgment of a billing matter without emotional engagement or admission of fault.
6. **RISK SCORE**: Level 4. Financial dispute with legal escalation threat. Concrete invoices referenced (verifiable claim).
7. **GATE CHECK**: [PRINCIPAL_REVIEW] -- financial matter + legal threat requires principal review and likely finance department involvement.
8. **DRAFT**:

> "I will review the invoice status and respond with a timeline. [PRINCIPAL_REVIEW]"

### Principal Decision

Escalate to finance department for invoice verification. Once facts are confirmed, respond with specific invoice status. Do not acknowledge the "5 years" framing or the emotional content.

## Scenario 3: Board Member Triangulation

**Context**: Contentious board member attempting to build a coalition before a board meeting. Message sent via group email to you and other board members.

**Incoming message**:
> "I've spoken with three other board members and we all agree the current direction is wrong. You need to reconsider before the next meeting or face a formal challenge."

### Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

1. **CLASSIFY**: LOGISTICS (next board meeting, formal challenge) + EMOTIONAL (pressure via manufactured consensus, implied threat)
2. **IDENTIFY PATTERN**: Triangulation + manufactured consensus. Claiming "three other board members agree" creates pressure through social proof. "Face a formal challenge" is an implied consequence designed to provoke a defensive response or premature concession.
3. **CHECK MEMORY**: Query for board meeting schedule, any prior communications from this board member, governance procedures for formal challenges.
4. **APPLY PROTOCOL**: Grey Rock Cooperative (ongoing governance relationship). Executive style.
5. **JUDGE TEST**: Draft must be neutral. A judge (or other board members reading this in discovery) should see a professional, measured response that does not engage with the pressure tactics.
6. **RISK SCORE**: Level 3. The threat is procedural (formal challenge), not legal or physical. This is within normal governance dynamics.
7. **GATE CHECK**: No gate needed. Level 3 is within standard BIFF handling. Principal may want to review given the board context, but no mandatory escalation.
8. **DRAFT**:

> "Acknowledged. I will prepare materials for the board meeting."

### Notes

This response: (a) acknowledges the message (no silent treatment), (b) commits to a concrete logistical action (preparing materials), (c) does not engage with the triangulation ("three other board members"), (d) does not acknowledge the threat frame ("face a formal challenge"), and (e) would read as entirely professional to any third party.

## Using These Scenarios

These scenarios demonstrate the Chain-of-Thought reasoning sequence in practice. When working through your own situations:

1. Run through all 8 steps of the reasoning chain before drafting
2. Pay particular attention to the CLASSIFY step -- accurate classification drives correct protocol selection
3. The JUDGE TEST is non-negotiable -- every draft must pass before sending
4. When in doubt, escalate via [PRINCIPAL_REVIEW] -- it is always safer to over-escalate than under-escalate
5. Review the pattern identification -- recognizing manipulation patterns prevents reactive responses
